# Judging Humor: Can a Language Model Predict the Joke?

### A literature review on humor, information theory, language models, and the edge of chaos

**Project framing.** This project asks whether a large language model (LLM) can *predict* jokes, and proposes that humor is, in information-theoretic terms, a phenomenon of **critical unpredictability** — a punchline that sits at the *edge of chaos* between the too-expected (boring) and the too-random (nonsense). The working signal is the model's own **surprisal / perplexity** at each token (the project's coinage *"epiplexity"*): the setup builds a confident expectation (low surprisal, narrowing entropy), and the punchline violates it (a sharp surprisal spike) in a way that can nonetheless be retroactively resolved. The empirical apparatus probes LLM activations for emotion/humor concept vectors (an EmotionScope-style replication) and aligns them against audience-laughter markers in stand-up transcripts (Bill Burr, John Mulaney).

This review situates that thesis across five literatures: (1) the philosophical and psychological **history of humor theory**; (2) **information theory and surprisal** in language; (3) **computational quantifications of humor**; (4) **computational humor and LLMs** (detection, understanding, generation, and laughter prediction); (5) **mechanistic interpretability** and (6) **criticality / edge of chaos**. The throughline is a single structural invariant that recurs across 2,400 years of theory: *humor is a disconfirmed prediction that is safely resolved* — a claim that information theory and modern LLMs finally let us measure.

## 1. A short history of humor theory: from superiority to disconfirmed prediction

Western theorizing about laughter begins with **superiority theory**. Plato (*Philebus*, *Republic*) treats the laughable as a mixed pleasure rooted in perceiving others' inferiority, and warns that unrestrained laughter undermines rational self-control. Aristotle (*Poetics*, *Nicomachean Ethics*) moderates this, defining the comic (the *ludicrous*) as "a failing or a piece of ugliness which causes no pain or destruction" — an early statement that the comic defect must be *harmless*, a condition that prefigures benign-violation theory two millennia later (Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy, "Philosophy of Humor"; Internet Encyclopedia of Philosophy, "Humor"). Hobbes (1651) gives superiority theory its most influential modern form: laughter as "sudden glory," a felt triumph over others or over one's own former self. Notably, the operative word is *sudden* — the response hinges on an abrupt, unplanned shift in appraisal, a cognate of the *unexpectedness* later formalized as surprisal.

The **relief / release** tradition reframes that suddenness energetically. Spencer (1860) proposes a hydraulic model in which laughter discharges surplus nervous energy along unused motor channels. Freud (1905) systematizes this into a theory of "psychic economy": jokes succeed by smuggling forbidden content past internal censorship, and the spared inhibitory energy is discharged as laughter. Freud's catalogue of joke *techniques* (condensation, displacement, double meaning, absurdity) is, structurally, a catalogue of ways to lead the listener down one interpretive path before abruptly revealing another — a build-and-release whose release point is the moment of maximal surprise.

The decisive turn is **incongruity theory**, which relocates the cause of laughter from social hierarchy to cognition. Beattie (1776) is the first to name *incongruity* explicitly: laughter arises from "two or more inconsistent, unsuitable, or incongruous parts or circumstances... united in one complex object." Kant (1790) gives the cleanest pre-computational statement: "laughter is an affection arising from the sudden transformation of a strained expectation into nothing." Schopenhauer (1818) makes the relation almost quantitative: laughter follows "the suddenly perceived incongruity between a concept and the real objects that have been thought through it," and its intensity scales with the degree and unexpectedness of the mismatch. In modern terms, the concept is a *prediction*, the percept is the *data*, and humor is high *prediction error*.

**Incongruity-resolution** models make the mechanism procedural. Suls (1972) proposes an explicitly information-processing, two-stage account: stage 1, the setup generates a prediction that the punchline violates (incongruity); stage 2, the perceiver finds a rule that makes the punchline make sense (resolution). Incongruity without resolution yields puzzlement; resolution without incongruity yields boredom; humor lives at their intersection. Shultz (1976) corroborates this developmentally — young children detect incongruity before they can execute the resolving inference. This two-stage structure maps directly onto an information-theoretic reading: stage 1 is a low-probability (high-surprisal) continuation; stage 2 is the entropy reduction of arriving at a coherent, if improbable, interpretation.

**Linguistic formalization** comes from Raskin (1985), whose Semantic Script Theory of Humor (SSTH) holds that a text is humorous when it is compatible with two opposed *scripts* (e.g., real/unreal, expected/unexpected, actual/non-actual), with humor arising at the *script switch*. Attardo and Raskin (1991) generalize SSTH into the General Theory of Verbal Humor (GTVH), adding six knowledge resources — Script Opposition, Logical Mechanism (the resolution operator), Situation, Target, Narrative Strategy, and Language — that together enable a formal similarity metric over jokes. Attardo (1997) shows that the cognitive (Suls/Shultz) and linguistic (SSTH/GTVH) traditions converge on the same mechanism — expectation (script activation) followed by violation (script opposition) — described at different levels. Morreall (1983) synthesizes superiority, relief, and incongruity, arguing that a "sudden pleasant psychological shift" is the unifying core.

Contemporary psychology refines the picture empirically. McGraw and Warren's (2010) **Benign Violation Theory** holds that humor requires a situation that is simultaneously a *violation* (of norms, expectations, or schemas) and *benign* (safe, distant, or low-stakes); neither alone suffices. Warren, Barsky, and McGraw's (2021) integrative review evaluates five candidate antecedents (surprise, simultaneity, superiority, violation, benign appraisal) and concludes that *violation* and *benign appraisal* co-occurring are necessary, while surprise alone is **not** independently sufficient. This is a crucial caution for any purely surprisal-based account: a punchline must be unexpected, but unexpectedness is a *necessary, not sufficient* ingredient — the *appraisal* of the violation (and its safety) does additional work that token probability does not capture.

## 2. Information theory and the surprisal of language

The mathematics underwriting "surprise = information" is Shannon's (1948). Entropy *H = −Σ p log p* measures average uncertainty; the **self-information** (surprisal) of an event is *−log p(x)*, so an improbable event carries high information when it occurs. This is the quantitative seed of every later surprisal account of language and humor.

In psycholinguistics, **surprisal theory** operationalizes processing difficulty as the surprisal of each word given its context. Hale (2001) derives this from a probabilistic parser; Levy (2008) generalizes it to comprehension at large: the difficulty of word *w* is *−log P(w | context)*, framing comprehension as Bayesian belief-updating whose cost is the prediction error. The linking hypothesis has strong empirical support: Smith and Levy (2013) show reading time is a *logarithmic* function of word probability; Wilcox et al. (2023) confirm the prediction across 11 languages using neural-LM surprisal; and Shain et al. (2024) replicate the logarithmic law at large scale with modern LMs. Neurally, Shain et al. (2020) find that surprisal modulates the brain's *language network* specifically (not the domain-general system), evidence that predictive coding is the substrate of comprehension.

This connects to the broader **predictive-processing** framework. Rao and Ballard (1999) propose hierarchical predictive coding: higher cortical areas send predictions downward, lower areas send prediction errors upward. Friston (2010) generalizes this to the **free-energy principle**: the brain acts to minimize a variational bound on surprise. Clark (2013) synthesizes these into the "prediction machine" view of mind. Under this lens, a joke is a controlled spike of free energy — a punchline that violates the generative model — followed by rapid resolution; the affective reward of "getting it" may signal successful model updating.

## 3. Quantifying humor as information

Several works close the loop between incongruity theory and Shannon's mathematics. Westbury, Shaoul, Moroschan, and Ramscar (2016) provide perhaps the most direct demonstration: the Shannon entropy of letter strings predicts how funny people find non-words — higher entropy (less predictable) reliably rates as funnier — empirically validating Schopenhauer's 200-year-old conjecture with information theory. Kao, Levy, and Goodman (2016) build a Bayesian model of sentence comprehension and define two information-theoretic measures of pun humor — **ambiguity** (entropy over meanings) and **distinctiveness** (KL-divergence between meaning distributions) — which jointly predict human funniness ratings for 435 puns. Hurley, Dennett, and Adams (2011), in *Inside Jokes*, give the cognitive-evolutionary version: humor is the reward signal for detecting and correcting a false commitment in the brain's generative model — i.e., prediction-error correction made pleasurable.

Most directly relevant to this project, Xie, Li, and Pu (2021) feed joke setups into GPT-2 and compute the setup's **uncertainty** (entropy of predicted continuations) and the punchline's **surprisal** (*−log p*); together these information-theoretic features outperform prior baselines on humor recognition. This is the closest published precedent to the project's *epiplexity* signal: it operationalizes the setup-as-anticipation / punchline-as-violation structure with an LM's own probabilities. Ma, Peng, Lyu, Zhang, and Zhu (2026) extend this to 828 Chinese stand-up performances with a *Dual Prediction Violation* framework, finding that LM surprisal interacts with **delivery timing** — pauses lengthen before high-surprisal punchlines, and peak (not average) semantic violation predicts appreciation. The lesson echoes Warren et al. (2021): surprisal is necessary but not sufficient, and timing mediates whether a spike of surprise actually lands as funny.

## 4. Computational humor and NLP

Computational humor began rule-based. Binsted and Ritchie's (1994, 1997) **JAPE** generates punning riddles from WordNet using phonological/semantic rules; Stock and Strapparava's (2003, 2005) **HAHAcronym** produces incongruous but plausible re-expansions of acronyms. Statistical humor *recognition* followed: Mihalcea and Strapparava (2005, 2006) trained classifiers on 16,000 one-liners vs. news, showing humor has detectable lexical/stylistic signatures; Yang, Lavie, Dyer, and Hovy (2015) added latent-structure features (incongruity, ambiguity) and an *anchor-extraction* method that localizes the humor-triggering phrase — a precursor to LM punchline localization.

A series of shared tasks standardized the field: SemEval-2017 Task 7 (pun detection/interpretation; Miller, Hempelmann, & Gurevych, 2017), SemEval-2017 Task 6 (#HashtagWars humor *ranking*; Potash, Romanov, & Rumshisky, 2017), and SemEval-2020 Task 7 (funniness *regression* on edited headlines / Humicroedit; Hossain, Krumm, Gamon, & Kautz, 2020). Transformer classifiers then pushed detection accuracy to near-ceiling on controlled data — Annamoradnejad and Zoghi's (2020) **ColBERT** reports ~98% F1 on a 200K joke/non-joke set. Gultchin, Patterson, Baym, Swinger, and Kalai (2019) showed humor-related word properties correspond to linear directions in embedding space — an early hint that humor may be a recoverable *direction* in representation, foreshadowing the interpretability work below.

The **New Yorker Caption Contest** became a flagship benchmark. Radev et al. (2015) first studied it computationally; Hessel et al. (2023), in *Do Androids Laugh at Electric Sheep?*, built three escalating tasks (match caption to cartoon, pick the winner, *explain* the joke) and showed even top vision-language and large language models lag far behind humans — especially at explanation. Zhang et al. (2024) released 250M+ human ratings over 2.2M captions, the largest humor-preference dataset, enabling reward-model-scale training.

## 5. LLMs and humor: detection, understanding, generation

The LLM era has produced a sobering consensus: models are fluent but not reliably funny, and detection outruns *understanding*. Jentzsch and Kersting (2023), *ChatGPT is fun, but it is not funny!*, find ChatGPT recycles a small set of cached jokes and shows shallow comprehension. Hessel et al. (2023) quantify the same gap on the New Yorker tasks. On the *tooling* side, Horvitz et al. (2024) use LLMs to generate *unfunny* contrastive counterparts, building paired datasets that improve humor classifiers. On *generation*, Tikhonov and Shtykovskiy (2024) show that decomposing joke creation into a multi-step chain (topic → setup → incongruity → punchline) raises rated funniness — externalizing exactly the surprise-resolution structure. Baluja (2024) argues *text is not all you need*: adding phonetic, rhythmic, and timing cues improves LLM humor understanding, implying that text-only perplexity misses prosody and delivery.

Closest to this project's data, Romanowski, Valois, and Fukui (2025), *From Punchlines to Predictions*, propose a metric for LLMs *identifying punchlines in stand-up transcripts*; ChatGPT/Claude/DeepSeek reach ~51% (with human agreement itself only ~41%), indicating that real-world stand-up punchline prediction is barely above chance even for frontier models. Two surveys frame the landscape: Amin and Burghardt (2020) on generation (flagging the absence of automatic funniness metrics as the field's central weakness), and Lemmens and De Marez (2026) as the most recent comprehensive state-of-the-art survey.

## 6. Predicting laughter

A parallel line predicts *when audiences laugh* from preceding context. Bertero and Fung (2016) train an LSTM on *Big Bang Theory* transcripts to predict laugh-track timing; Chen and Lee (2017) predict TED-talk laughter with a CNN over lexical features. Recent multimodal corpora make the problem richer and more naturalistic: TIC-TALK (Zribi, Cafiero, Lépinay, & Vidal-Gorène, 2026) aligns text, audio, laughter, and kinesics over 90 filmed stand-up sets; StandUp4AI (Barriere et al., 2025) provides 330+ hours of stand-up across seven languages with humor annotations. These resources are the natural targets for testing whether an LM-surprisal signal predicts actual laughter events — precisely this project's setup with Burr and Mulaney transcripts.

## 7. Mechanistic interpretability: looking for humor inside the model

If humor is a recoverable *signal* inside an LLM, interpretability supplies the toolkit. The **circuits** program (Olah et al., 2020) and the transformer-circuits framework (Elhage et al., 2021) decompose model computation into human-readable features and algorithms; induction heads (Olsson et al., 2022) are the prediction-completion mechanism whose violation at a punchline is what surprisal measures. **Superposition** (Elhage et al., 2022) explains why raw neurons are polysemantic, motivating **sparse autoencoders** that recover monosemantic features — first on a toy model (Bricken et al., 2023) and then at production scale on Claude 3 Sonnet, where 34M features include abstract emotional and conceptual ones (Templeton et al., 2024).

A complementary **linear-representation** literature holds that high-level concepts are directions in activation space (Park, Choe, & Veitch, 2023), exemplified by space/time "neurons" (Gurnee & Tegmark, 2023). This grounds *concept-vector* methods: Representation Engineering (Zou et al., 2023) and Activation Addition (Turner et al., 2023) extract and steer linear directions for high-level concepts including emotion. Most directly, emotion-specific interpretability has matured rapidly: Tak et al. (2025) probe emotion inference across layers; Wang et al. (2025) discover and causally control emotion *circuits*; and Sofroniew et al. (2026), *Emotion concepts and their function in a large language model*, recover linear **emotion vectors** that activate appropriately, steer behavior, and decouple internal from expressed state — the work most directly analogous to this project's EmotionScope-style probing.

Crucially for an *epiplexity* account, transformers contain identifiable machinery for **prediction uncertainty**: Gurnee et al. (2024) catalogue "entropy neurons" that modulate output-distribution entropy without changing the predicted token, and Stolfo et al. (2024) give a mechanistic account of "confidence-regulation" neurons that scale logits via the unembedding null space. These are the clearest internal analogues of the surprisal signal that incongruity-resolution theory predicts should spike at a punchline.

## 8. Criticality and the edge of chaos

The project's "edge of chaos" framing draws on a deep physics/biology lineage. Bak, Tang, and Wiesenfeld (1987) introduced **self-organized criticality** — systems that spontaneously evolve to a critical state with power-law (1/f) event distributions, poised between frozen order and turbulence at the point of maximal sensitivity and information transmission. Langton (1990) showed computationally that information storage, transmission, and modification are jointly maximized near the order–chaos phase transition — "computation at the edge of chaos." Biologically, Beggs and Plenz (2003) found cortical "neuronal avalanches" with the power-law signature of criticality, and Shew and Plenz (2013) reviewed evidence that dynamic range and information capacity are maximized at criticality — the functional case for brains operating near the critical point.

The same idea appears in deep learning: Poole et al. (2016) and Schoenholz et al. (2017) show networks achieve maximal expressivity and trainable signal propagation at the "edge of chaos" initialization, while Kaplan et al.'s (2020) neural scaling laws reveal power-law behavior echoing the Zipfian, scale-free statistics of natural language. Connecting criticality to *aesthetics*, Berlyne (1971) proposed that hedonic response follows an inverted-U over stimulus complexity/uncertainty — peaking at intermediate novelty — and Bilder and Knudsen (2014) extended the edge-of-chaos framing to creative cognition, where ideas must be simultaneously novel (surprising) and coherent (resolvable). The most rigorous modern confirmation comes from music: Gold, Pearce, Mas-Herrero, Dagher, and Zatorre (2019) used information-theoretic modeling of real musical excerpts to show that *both* unpredictability and uncertainty exert inverted-U (Wundt) effects on liking — accounting for 19–42% of preference variance across two samples — while engaging dopaminergic reward, casting musical pleasure as "a reward for learning" maximized at intermediate predictive challenge. This is precisely the dual constraint a good punchline satisfies: maximally surprising yet still resolvable. It also supplies a candidate *resolution* term for the humor account — the affective reward of successful uncertainty reduction (cf. Friston, 2010; Hurley et al., 2011) — and a concrete, transferable method for testing whether funniness, like musical liking, *peaks* at an intermediate level of LM-measured surprisal rather than rising monotonically with it.

## 9. Synthesis and research gaps

Across 2,400 years, humor theory converges on one structural invariant: **a safely-resolved disconfirmed prediction**. Superiority and relief theories captured the *suddenness*; incongruity theory (Beattie → Kant → Schopenhauer) located the cause in failed cognitive prediction; incongruity-resolution (Suls, Shultz) and script theory (Raskin, Attardo) made it procedural and formal; benign-violation theory (McGraw & Warren) added the safety constraint. Information theory supplies the measure: surprisal (*−log p*) quantifies the violation, and an unbroken empirical chain (Hale, Levy, Smith & Levy, Wilcox, Shain) shows LM surprisal tracks human processing. Westbury et al. (2016), Kao et al. (2016), and Xie et al. (2021) demonstrate that information-theoretic quantities derived from language models predict funniness — and mechanistic interpretability locates *internal* uncertainty machinery (entropy/confidence neurons) and recoverable emotion/concept directions (Sofroniew et al., 2026; Zou et al., 2023) that this project's probes can target.

**What is well established.** (a) Surprisal is a valid, cross-lingual proxy for human linguistic prediction effort. (b) LM-derived surprisal/entropy correlates with perceived funniness for puns, one-liners, and headlines. (c) Brains and deep networks process information optimally near criticality, and aesthetic liking peaks at *intermediate* predictive complexity (the inverted-U; Berlyne, 1971; Gold et al., 2019). (d) LLMs detect humor far better than they *understand* or *time* it.

**What is speculative — and where this project contributes.** No prior work formally unifies these into the claim that *humor requires punchline surprisal to sit at a critical point — the edge of chaos between the too-predictable and the too-random — and that an LLM's own perplexity operationalizes that critical point*. The free-energy principle (Friston, 2010) and predictive-brain account (Clark, 2013) provide the theoretical bridge from criticality to affective reward, but the explicit "joke = critical unpredictability, measured by LM perplexity" formalization remains open. Three concrete gaps frame the contribution:

1. **Sufficiency.** Surprisal is necessary but not sufficient (Warren et al., 2021; Ma et al., 2026). A criticality account must specify the *resolution* term (an entropy-reduction or benign-appraisal gate), not just the violation magnitude — otherwise random text would be maximally "funny."
2. **Modality and timing.** Text-only perplexity misses prosody, delivery, and timing that drive laughter (Baluja, 2024; Ma et al., 2026); aligning surprisal with actual laughter markers in stand-up (this project's design, and the TIC-TALK / StandUp4AI corpora) is the way to test it.
3. **Internal mechanism.** Whether the model's *representation* of humor (a concept/emotion direction, or an entropy/confidence-neuron signature) predicts laughter better than its output perplexity is an open, directly testable question — the bridge between the surprisal account and mechanistic interpretability that this project is positioned to build.
